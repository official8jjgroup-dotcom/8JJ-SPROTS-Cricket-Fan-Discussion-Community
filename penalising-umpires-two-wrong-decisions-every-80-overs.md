# Penalising Umpires: Two Wrong Decisions Every 80 Overs?

Cricket’s debate over umpire accountability is intensifying, with growing support for a proposal that would penalise umpires who make two incorrect decisions every 80 overs. The idea—gaining traction among players, experts, and fans—aims to strengthen officiating standards and reinforce the integrity of the sport.

---

## The Case for Umpire Accountability

Cricket is built on precision, timing, and split-second decisions. Umpiring mistakes, especially in crucial moments, can significantly alter the outcome of a match. Supporters of the proposal argue that:

- Repeated errors undermine the credibility of the game  
- Penalties would create incentives for sharper focus and improved decision-making  
- DRS has reduced errors, but human judgment remains a major factor  

Former players note that despite advanced technology, the pressure on umpires is immense. Accountability measures, they argue, could enhance professionalism and consistency.

---

## How the System Could Work

The proposed model would track umpire performance across formats:

- **Two incorrect decisions within 80 overs** triggers a review  
- Based on severity & frequency, consequences may include:
  - Written warnings  
  - Temporary suspensions  
  - Removal from future international fixtures  
- Reviews would be supported by:
  - Match referees  
  - Third-umpire oversight  
  - DRS analytics  

This approach aligns with evaluation systems used in other global sports where officials undergo routine grading.

---

## Potential Challenges and Criticism

Not everyone supports the proposal. Critics argue:

- Increased pressure may **cause more mistakes**, not fewer  
- “Wrong decisions” can be **subjective**, especially borderline calls  
- Overturned decisions via DRS do not always indicate incompetence  
- Technology should **assist**, not penalise officials  

The mental toll on umpires is also a concern, given the already-high stress of elite cricket.

---

## Global Cricket Governance Perspective

The ICC has traditionally focused on:

- Enhanced umpire training  
- Better access to technology  
- Standardised review procedures  

However, discussions about performance-based accountability are circulating as cricket aims to modernise its governance structures. Supporters believe such systems could help filter top-tier umpires for major tournaments like World Cups, ensuring consistent officiating standards.

---

## Looking Ahead

Whether or not penalties become official policy, the discussion signals a shift in how the cricket world views officiating. Fans and players increasingly demand transparent and consistent decision-making.

As the sport evolves, frameworks like these may redefine the role and expectations of umpires—balancing human judgment with technological precision while maintaining trust in the game’s integrity.
